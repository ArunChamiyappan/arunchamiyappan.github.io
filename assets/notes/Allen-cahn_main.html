Allen-Cahn Equation

The Allen–Cahn equation is a phase-field model that describes the temporal evolution of an order parameter \(\phi(\mathbf{x},t)\), which distinguishes between different phases of a material. It was originally proposed to model the motion of anti-phase boundaries in alloys.

Free EnergyFunctional

The model is based on a total free energy functional: \[F[\phi] = \int_\Omega \left( \frac{\epsilon^2}{2} |\nabla \phi|^2 + W(\phi) \right) \, d\mathbf{x},\] where

  • \(\epsilon\) controls the interface width (gradient energy coefficient),

  • \(W(\phi)\) is a double-wellpotential. Here we choose \[W(\phi) = \phi (1 - \phi)(1 - 2\phi),\] which favors two stable states: \(\phi = 0\) and \(\phi = 1\).

Evolution Equation

The Allen–Cahn equation is derived using time-dependent Ginzburg–Landau dynamics: \[\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial t} = -M \frac{\delta F}{\delta \phi},\] where \(M\) is the phase-field mobility.

Evaluating the variational derivative gives: \[\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial t} = M \left( \epsilon^2 \nabla^2 \phi - \frac{dW}{d\phi} \right).\]

For the chosen potential, \[\begin{aligned} W(\phi) &= \phi - 3\phi^2 + 2\phi^3, \\[6pt] \frac{dW}{d\phi} &= 1 - 6\phi + 6\phi^2. \end{aligned}\]

Thus, the Allen–Cahn equation becomes \[\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial t} = M \left( \epsilon^2 \nabla^2 \phi - \left( 1 - 6\phi + 6\phi^2 \right) \right).\]

Physical Interpretation

  • The diffusion-like term \(\epsilon^2 \nabla^2 \phi\) smooths the interface (surface tension effect).

  • The reaction term \(-\big(1 - 6\phi + 6\phi^2\big)\) drives the system toward the bulk phases (\ \(\phi = 0, 1\)).

  • The equation models interface motion driven by curvature.
